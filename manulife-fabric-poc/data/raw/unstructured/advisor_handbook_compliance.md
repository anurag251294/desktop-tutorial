## 1. Purpose

This handbook establishes the compliance standards and expectations for all advisors distributing Manulife insurance and investment products in Canada. Adherence to these standards is mandatory and is a condition of maintaining your distribution agreement with Manulife. Non-compliance may result in corrective action, including suspension or termination of your distribution agreement.

This handbook is supplemental to, and does not replace, the regulatory obligations imposed by your licensing body, the applicable self-regulatory organization (SRO), and federal and provincial legislation. Advisors are responsible for understanding and complying with all applicable regulatory requirements.

---

## 2. Regulatory Framework

Advisors distributing Manulife products operate within a multi-layered regulatory environment:

### 2.1 Federal Regulators
- **Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)**: Prudential regulator of Manulife as a federally regulated life insurance company. OSFI guidelines, including Guideline E-13 (Legislative Compliance Management) and Guideline B-10 (Outsourcing), establish expectations for the management of distribution relationships.
- **Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC)**: Enforces consumer protection provisions applicable to financial institutions.

### 2.2 Provincial Insurance Regulators
- Advisors selling insurance products must be licensed by the applicable provincial insurance regulator (e.g., Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario (FSRA), Autorité des marchés financiers (AMF) in Quebec, Financial Services Commission of British Columbia).
- Licensing requirements include completion of the Life License Qualification Program (LLQP) or the Quebec equivalent, successful completion of qualifying examinations, and ongoing continuing education.

### 2.3 Securities Regulation
- Advisors selling segregated funds, mutual funds, or other securities must be registered with the applicable provincial securities commission and be a member of the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO), formerly the Mutual Fund Dealers Association (MFDA) and Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC).
- The Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA), through National Instruments and Companion Policies, establish harmonized rules across provinces.

### 2.4 Key Regulatory References
- National Instrument 31-103: Registration Requirements, Exemptions and Ongoing Registrant Obligations
- National Instrument 81-105: Mutual Fund Sales Practices
- CSA Staff Notice 31-336: Guidance for Portfolio Managers, Exempt Market Dealers and Other Registrants on the Know-Your-Client, Know-Your-Product and Suitability Obligations
- CLHIA Guideline G2: Individual Variable Insurance Contracts Relating to Segregated Funds

## 3. Know Your Client (KYC) Requirements

### 3.1 Obligation

Before making any recommendation or accepting any order for an insurance or investment product, the advisor must gather sufficient information about the client to assess suitability. This is both a regulatory requirement and a Manulife policy requirement.

### 3.2 Information to Collect

The following information must be collected and documented for every client:

**Personal Information:**
- Full legal name, date of birth, and Social Insurance Number (SIN)
- Address, telephone number, and email address
- Citizenship, residency status, and tax residency (for CRS and FATCA compliance)
- Employment status, occupation, and employer name
- Marital/family status and number of dependants

**Financial Information:**
- Annual income (gross and net)
- Net worth (assets less liabilities)
- Liquid net worth
- Sources of income
- Existing insurance coverage (life, health, disability)
- Existing investment accounts and approximate values
- Outstanding debts and liabilities
- Tax bracket

**Investment Profile (for investment products):**
- Investment objectives (capital preservation, income, balanced growth, growth, aggressive growth)
- Risk tolerance (low, low-medium, medium, medium-high, high)
- Time horizon (short-term, medium-term, long-term)
- Investment knowledge and experience
- Liquidity needs

**Insurance Needs (for insurance products):**
- Purpose of coverage (income replacement, mortgage protection, estate planning, business continuity)
- Desired coverage amount and term
- Existing coverage and gaps
- Health status and family medical history (as disclosed in the application)
- Smoking status

### 3.3 KYC Updates

KYC information must be updated:
- At least every 36 months (triennial review)
- Whenever there is a material change in the client's circumstances (e.g., marriage, divorce, retirement, significant change in income or net worth)
- Before any new transaction or recommendation
- Upon the client's request

### 3.4 Documentation

All KYC information must be documented on the Manulife KYC form (or the dealer's approved KYC form) and retained in the client file. Electronic records are acceptable provided they meet Manulife's information security standards. The client must sign the KYC form, and a copy must be provided to the client.

## 4. Suitability Assessment

### 4.1 Obligation

Every recommendation made to a client must be suitable based on the client's KYC information. The advisor must be able to demonstrate that each recommendation considers the client's:
- Financial circumstances and needs
- Investment objectives and risk tolerance
- Time horizon
- Existing portfolio composition
- Tax situation

### 4.2 Suitability Framework

Manulife has adopted the following suitability framework, which is consistent with CSA and CIRO requirements:

**Step 1 - Know Your Product (KYP):** The advisor must have a thorough understanding of the product being recommended, including its features, benefits, risks, costs, and any restrictions. Advisors may only recommend products on their firm's approved product shelf.

**Step 2 - Match Product to Client:** The advisor must match the product's characteristics to the client's KYC profile. Key considerations include:
- Does the product align with the client's stated investment objectives?
- Is the product's risk rating consistent with the client's risk tolerance?
- Is the product appropriate given the client's time horizon?
- Are the costs reasonable relative to the expected benefit?
- Does the product create undue concentration in the client's overall portfolio?

**Step 3 - Document the Rationale:** The advisor must document the rationale for each recommendation in the client file. This is especially important for:
- Recommendations that differ from the client's stated objectives or risk tolerance
- Leveraged investment recommendations
- Recommendations involving complex products (e.g., segregated funds with guarantee features, universal life insurance)
- Replacement of existing policies or products

**Step 4 - Obtain Client Acknowledgement:** The client must acknowledge that they have received and understood the recommendation. For investment products, the client must sign the trade instructions. For insurance products, the client must sign the application.

### 4.3 Unsuitable Transactions

If a client insists on a transaction that the advisor believes is unsuitable, the advisor must:
1. Explain the reasons why the transaction is not recommended
2. Document the discussion and the client's insistence in writing
3. Obtain the client's written acknowledgement that they are proceeding against the advisor's recommendation
4. Escalate to the advisor's compliance department if the transaction poses significant risk to the client

### 4.4 Leveraged Investing

Recommendations involving borrowed money to invest (leveraging) require enhanced suitability assessment and documentation. The advisor must:
- Ensure the client fully understands the risks of leveraging, including the possibility of losing more than the original investment
- Document the client's ability to service the debt independent of investment returns
- Obtain the client's signed Leveraged Investing Acknowledgement form
- Not recommend leveraging to clients with a conservative risk tolerance or short time horizon

## 5. Documentation Standards

### 5.1 Client File Requirements

Every client file must contain, at minimum:
- Signed KYC form (current within 36 months)
- Copies of all applications and policy documents
- Needs analysis or financial plan documentation
- Suitability rationale for each recommendation
- All correspondence with the client (letters, emails, file notes of verbal communications)
- Copies of disclosure documents provided (Fund Facts, policy illustrations, fee schedules)
- Signed client acknowledgement forms

### 5.2 File Notes

Advisors must maintain contemporaneous file notes documenting all material interactions with clients, including:
- Date and method of communication (in-person, phone, email, virtual meeting)
- Summary of discussion topics
- Recommendations made and rationale
- Client's response and decisions
- Any concerns or complaints raised by the client

File notes must be factual, objective, and professional. They should be created within 24 hours of the interaction.

### 5.3 Record Retention

Client files and all associated records must be retained for a minimum of 7 years from the date of the last transaction, or 7 years from the date the client relationship ends, whichever is later. For insurance policies, records must be retained for 7 years after the policy terminates. Electronic records must be stored in compliance with Manulife's Information Security Policy and backed up regularly.

## 6. Conflict of Interest Policies

### 6.1 Identification and Disclosure

Advisors must identify and disclose all material conflicts of interest to clients, including:
- Compensation arrangements (commissions, trailing commissions, bonuses, incentive trips)
- Referral arrangements and referral fees
- Personal financial interests in recommended products or companies
- Family relationships with other financial professionals involved in the client's affairs
- Any situation where the advisor's interests may diverge from the client's interests

### 6.2 Compensation Disclosure

In accordance with the Client Relationship Model (CRM2) and CSA requirements, advisors must disclose to clients:
- The nature and amount of compensation received from each product recommended
- Trailing commission amounts, reported annually on client statements
- Any non-monetary benefits received from product manufacturers (subject to the restrictions in NI 81-105)

### 6.3 Prohibited Practices

The following practices are strictly prohibited:
- Recommending products primarily to maximize the advisor's compensation
- Churning (excessive trading or policy replacement for the purpose of generating commissions)
- Accepting gifts or entertainment from product manufacturers that exceed $500 in aggregate per year per manufacturer
- Participating in undisclosed outside business activities
- Borrowing from or lending to clients
- Acting as executor, trustee, or power of attorney for a client (except for family members)
- Personal trading based on non-public information about client transactions

### 6.4 Outside Business Activities

Advisors must disclose all outside business activities to Manulife and their dealer, including:
- Employment or business activities outside of their advisory practice
- Board memberships
- Positions held with other financial institutions
- Any activity that could create a conflict of interest or impact the advisor's ability to fulfill their obligations

## 7. Continuing Education Requirements

### 7.1 Regulatory CE Requirements

Advisors must complete continuing education (CE) as required by their licensing body:

| License Type | CE Requirement |
|---|---|
| Life Insurance (LLQP) | Varies by province (e.g., Ontario: 30 credits per 2-year cycle, including 15 professional development and 5 compliance) |
| Mutual Fund (CIRO) | 30 CE credits per 2-year cycle, including mandatory compliance and product knowledge components |
| Securities (CIRO) | As prescribed by CIRO member rules |

### 7.2 Manulife-Specific Training

In addition to regulatory CE, Manulife requires advisors to complete the following annually:
- **Annual Compliance Training**: Mandatory online course covering anti-money laundering (AML), privacy, cyber security, and current compliance topics. Must be completed by March 31 each year.
- **Product Training**: Advisors must complete product-specific training before selling any new Manulife product. Training records are maintained by Manulife's Learning & Development team.
- **Anti-Money Laundering (AML) Training**: Annual refresher training on Manulife's obligations under the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA). Topics include client identification, suspicious transaction reporting, and politically exposed persons (PEP) screening.

### 7.3 Consequences of Non-Compliance

Failure to complete mandatory training by the specified deadline will result in:
- First occurrence: Written warning and 30-day extension
- Second occurrence: Suspension of new business privileges until training is completed
- Third occurrence: Termination of distribution agreement

## 8. Anti-Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing

### 8.1 Obligations

As a reporting entity under the PCMLTFA, Manulife and its advisors must:
- Verify client identity using reliable, independent source documents before opening an account or processing a transaction
- Determine whether the client is a Politically Exposed Person (PEP), Head of an International Organization (HIO), or a family member or close associate of such a person
- Report suspicious transactions to Manulife's AML Compliance team (who will file Suspicious Transaction Reports with FINTRAC as required)
- Report large cash transactions ($10,000 or more) to Manulife's AML Compliance team
- Maintain records of all identity verification documents and transaction records

### 8.2 Client Identity Verification

Acceptable identity verification documents include:
- Government-issued photo identification (passport, driver's licence, provincial photo card)
- For entities: Articles of incorporation, partnership agreements, trust agreements
- Dual-process method: Using two independent, reliable sources to verify name, date of birth, and address

## 9. Complaint Handling

### 9.1 Advisor's Responsibility

When a client expresses dissatisfaction (verbally or in writing), the advisor must:
1. Acknowledge the complaint promptly (within 1 business day)
2. Document the complaint in the client file, including the date, nature of the complaint, and the client's desired resolution
3. Report the complaint to their dealer's compliance department within 2 business days
4. Cooperate fully with the complaint investigation
5. Not attempt to resolve the complaint independently without involving compliance, unless it is a minor service issue that can be resolved immediately

### 9.2 Escalation

All written complaints must be reported to Manulife's Compliance team at compliance@manulife.com. Manulife will investigate and respond to the complainant in writing within 90 days, as required by CIRO rules and insurance regulatory guidelines.
