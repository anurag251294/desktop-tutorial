# Manulife Investment Management Quarterly Commentary

**Q1 2026 (January 1 - March 31, 2026)**
**Prepared by: Manulife Investment Management, Global Asset Allocation Team**
**Date: April 10, 2026**

## Market Overview

The first quarter of 2026 was characterized by cautious optimism across global markets, tempered by persistent geopolitical uncertainty and evolving central bank policy. After a volatile start to the year driven by renewed trade tensions between the United States and China, equity markets recovered in February and March as corporate earnings broadly exceeded expectations and inflation data continued its gradual decline.

In Canada, the Bank of Canada held its overnight rate steady at 3.25% throughout the quarter, citing balanced risks between slowing economic growth and still-elevated services inflation. The Canadian economy grew at an annualized rate of 1.4% in Q1, with strength in the technology and mining sectors partially offset by weakness in residential construction. The Canadian dollar traded in a narrow range, ending the quarter at 1.37 against the U.S. dollar.

The U.S. Federal Reserve delivered one 25 basis-point rate cut in March, bringing the federal funds rate to 4.00-4.25%. U.S. GDP growth remained resilient at 2.1% annualized, supported by consumer spending and a strong labour market, though manufacturing activity contracted for the third consecutive month. The S&P 500 returned 4.8% in the quarter, led by the technology and healthcare sectors.

European markets performed well, with the Euro Stoxx 50 returning 5.2% as the European Central Bank cut rates twice in the quarter, responding to slowing growth and inflation falling below 2% for the first time since 2021. The UK economy remained in a shallow recession, and the Bank of England held rates unchanged amid mixed economic signals.

Emerging markets were mixed. Chinese equities rallied 7.3% on stimulus measures and improved property sector sentiment, while Latin American markets struggled with commodity price weakness and political uncertainty in Brazil and Mexico.

Fixed income markets benefited from the rate-cutting cycle. The FTSE Canada Universe Bond Index returned 2.1% in the quarter, with long-duration bonds outperforming as the yield curve steepened modestly. Corporate credit spreads tightened by 15 basis points, reflecting improved risk appetite.

## Outlook and Positioning

### Equities

We maintain a constructive outlook on equities for the remainder of 2026, though we expect returns to moderate from the strong pace of 2024-2025. Corporate earnings growth is decelerating but remains positive, and valuations in most markets are within historical norms. We see the following key themes shaping our equity positioning:

**Canada**: We favour energy infrastructure, critical minerals, and technology companies. The Canadian banking sector faces headwinds from slowing mortgage growth and rising provisions for credit losses, and we have reduced our financial sector weighting. We are overweight in Canadian industrials benefiting from fiscal infrastructure spending.

**United States**: We have shifted our U.S. equity positioning towards more defensive sectors, including healthcare and consumer staples, while maintaining selective exposure to AI-related technology companies. Valuations in the U.S. remain elevated relative to international markets, and we see better risk-adjusted opportunities abroad.

**International**: We are modestly overweight European equities, particularly in the industrials and banking sectors, which offer attractive valuations and benefit from ECB easing. We have increased our allocation to Japanese equities, supported by corporate governance reforms and a weak yen benefiting exporters.

**Emerging Markets**: We maintain a neutral allocation, with a preference for India and Southeast Asia over China and Latin America. While Chinese stimulus is supportive, structural challenges in the property sector and geopolitical risks warrant caution.

### Fixed Income

We expect the Bank of Canada to deliver two additional 25 basis-point rate cuts in the second half of 2026, bringing the overnight rate to 2.75% by year-end. The U.S. Federal Reserve is expected to cut once more in Q3. This environment is supportive for fixed income, and we maintain a modestly long duration position in our bond funds.

Within credit, we continue to favour investment-grade corporate bonds, particularly in the 5-10 year maturity range, where spreads offer adequate compensation for credit risk. We have reduced high yield exposure modestly, as spreads have tightened to levels that do not fully compensate for the risk of an economic slowdown.

### Alternative Assets

Real estate investment trusts (REITs) are expected to benefit from declining interest rates, and we have increased our REIT allocation. Industrial and residential REITs remain our preferred sub-sectors, while we continue to underweight office REITs given structural headwinds from remote work trends.

## Key Risks

The following risks could negatively impact investment performance and are being actively monitored by our investment team:

1. **Geopolitical escalation**: Renewed U.S.-China trade tensions, the ongoing conflict in Eastern Europe, and Middle East instability could disrupt supply chains and dampen investor sentiment.

2. **Inflation resurgence**: While headline inflation has declined, services inflation remains sticky. An unexpected resurgence could force central banks to pause or reverse rate cuts, negatively impacting both equity and fixed income markets.

3. **Canadian housing correction**: Canadian household debt-to-income ratios remain among the highest in the G7. A sharp correction in housing prices could trigger consumer retrenchment and bank credit losses, with broad economic consequences.

4. **Credit deterioration**: Corporate leverage has increased during the low-rate era. A slowing economy could lead to rising defaults, particularly in the high yield and leveraged loan markets.

5. **AI valuation risk**: Significant capital has flowed into AI-related equities, and valuations in parts of the technology sector appear stretched. A disappointment in AI monetization timelines could trigger a correction.

6. **Regulatory changes**: Proposed changes to Canadian capital gains taxation and financial sector regulation could impact investment returns and the financial services industry.

## Conclusion

The first quarter of 2026 reinforced the importance of diversification and disciplined asset allocation. While markets have delivered solid returns, we believe investors should remain balanced and avoid concentrated bets in any single region or sector. Our investment team continues to focus on identifying high-quality companies with durable competitive advantages, maintaining appropriate portfolio diversification, and managing downside risk through prudent fixed income and alternative asset positioning.

We encourage investors to review their financial plans regularly with their Manulife financial advisor to ensure their portfolios remain aligned with their long-term goals and risk tolerance.
